In November, Loveland voters will be asked this ballot question:

Without increasing taxes, and to restore local authority, shall the City of Loveland be authorized to provide advanced service (high speed internet), cable television service, and telecommunications service, directly or indirectly with public or private sector partners, to residents, businesses, schools, health care facilities, libraries, nonprofit entities, and other users of such services located within the boundaries of the City of Loveland and the service territory of the City of Loveland’s power enterprise as expressly permitted by Title 29, Article 27 of the Colorado Revised Statutes?

What's this about? Why are we being asked to vote on this issue?

The Colorado Legislature in 2005 passed a measure, SB-152, limiting local governments from competing with the private sector to provide high-speed internet, telecommunication and cable services. The law prohibits the City from providing these services directly or indirectly, with some very limited exceptions.

But along with its prohibitions, SB-152 contains a provision that permits voter approval to give their communities the opportunity to engage in providing high-speed, broadband internet services. In short, we can decide to take back the right that was taken away from the City with passage of SB-152.

What is broadband? Why should we be interested?

We're really talking about "fiber-optic broadband", which uses lasers to send information through glass fibers. This provides the highest internet performance, with speeds 10 to 100 times faster than commercial carriers in Loveland generally provide through conventional cable, telephone and wireless networks.

A community fiber optic network is an information superhighway that runs right to your home or office. Two or more internet service providers can offer competing plans over the same fiber network, just like multiple trucking firms can deliver packages over the same road system.

The City has access to many miles of fiber-optic cable that is currently unused – “dark fiber,” as it is called. That resource could provide the starting point for a community-wide, fiber-optic broadband system. If the voters demonstrate interest by approving the ballot measure, the city could explore ways to provide broadband service.

What happens if voters approve this ballot measure?

Voter approval restores local authority, allowing the conversation to begin. Passage would allow our community to study whether providing fiber-optic broadband service, either independently or in a public-private partnership, is feasible, affordable and in the interest of citizens. The people of Loveland, through their elected representatives, would determine the next course of action.

This vote does NOT authorize a specific plan for providing fiber-optic broadband. There are many ways that might be done, and cities across the country have taken a variety of approaches. Other recent Loveland development projects have been done as public/private partnerships.

We need to figure out what's best for Loveland, but Council can't investigate this until we vote ourselves the right to do so.

Have people in other communities voted on this issue?

Yes. Voters in almost a dozen Colorado towns and cities have exempted themselves from the law’s prohibitions, affirming local choice in decisions about broadband service. For example, 92 percent of Estes Park voters in a special election in February cast ballots that reasserted local authority.

Longmont is already building out their NextLight network, using fiber previously laid by the power company. Their website describes Longmont as a "Gigabyte City", with the fastest internet service in the world. Fort Collins will be voting in November on a ballot issue very similar to ours.

Connect Loveland! It's about opportunity.

Today, Loveland is not well connected. Downtown merchants are stuck with slow, old fashioned DSL service over phone lines. Our school internet connections are so inadequate that if most students accessed coursework or took tests using iPad tablets, the school networks would be completely unable to handle the workload. Even the City's own Wi-Fi service in Council Chambers is quite slow. Accessing high-resolution medical images, such as CT and MRI scans, is slow unless you're actually in hospital or adjacent offices with their own fiber-optic connections. Media and entertainment services are migrating from cable and satellite TV to the internet.

For individuals, this vote could enable more competition to provide Loveland with world-class internet services at better prices. Education is going global, with students accessing the very best course materials and instructors wherever they are in the world. Our students should be sharing that global stage. It's an opportunity for all of us to connect faster more reliably to the things we love, at a more competitive cost.

This is also an opportunity to retain our existing businesses in Loveland, and help them grow. And the new businesses that we hope to attract will require world-class information services. With the right connections, even small businesses can go global.

There will be fiber broadband "have" cities, and "have-nots". Loveland must not become a little pocket of have-nots, surrounded by communities in Estes Park, Longmont, and Fort Collins with vastly faster, more reliable, less expensive internet service.

Who is Made in Loveland?

Made in Loveland is an independent group of volunteers working to foster business and educational opportunities in technology and the arts. You already know us as the artists, engineers, scientists, educators, entrepreneurs and philanthropists behind the Loveland CreatorSpace.

This campaign is a local effort, by local people who have Loveland's best interests at heart. Our city has a long history of making great things happen through community involvement. High-speed internet access is so important for our future that it really is a community issue. It's an opportunity we shouldn't miss.
